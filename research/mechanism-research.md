## THE PROBLEM MECHANISM (UMP): Biofilm Shield

### What Is Biofilm?

A biofilm is a group of bacteria that stick together and attach to a surface, like the walls of the vagina. To protect themselves, they create a slimy layer that acts like a shield.

**Key facts:**
- At its simplest, a biofilm is a community of microorganisms living together in a self-made 3D structure
- This structure — made of an extracellular polymeric substance (or EPS) — protects the bacteria living inside
- It is really good at sticking to surfaces (like your vagina's epithelial wall)
- Once a biofilm forms, the infection becomes embedded, providing a physical and chemical barrier

### Why Standard Treatments Fail

**1. Antibiotic Resistance**
- Once a biofilm is established, the microbes inside can become up to 1,000 times more tolerant to antibiotics or antifungals
- Bacteria within biofilms are not effectively eliminated by the immune system or fully destroyed by antibiotics
- BV tends to have a high rate of relapse and recurrence because biofilm-related infections persist

**2. The Recurrence Problem**
- 58% of women with BV experience recurrence within 12 months of treatment
- G. vaginalis biofilm displays high resistance to protective mechanisms of normal vaginal microflora
- Hydrogen peroxide and lactic acid produced by lactobacilli can't penetrate

### The Codename: "Biofilm Shield"

Simple explanation for copy:
> "The bacteria causing your symptoms don't just float around. They build something called a biofilm — basically a protective shield that antibiotics and regular probiotics can't penetrate."
